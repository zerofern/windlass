B. Character Sentences (CS)
What to track: Sentences that convey essential characterization cues. How to mine them: This requires a specialized NLP pipeline integrating Large Language Models (LLMs). First, segment the text into distinct sentences

C. Relation Arcs
What to track: The dynamic, shifting emotional circumstances and interactions between directed pairs of characters as the plot unfolds across a sequence of events

How to mine them: First, utilize an NLP tool like BookNLP to extract character entities, followed by an event tagger (like a BiLSTM model) to extract specific narrative events

The 5x5 Grid Approach: You can structure the Circumplex inputs into a 5-level scale for each dimension. For instance, Pleasure is scored from P1 (very displeased) to P5 (very pleased), and Activeness is scored from A1 (very inactive) to A5 (very active)

please describe the  Circumplex Model

The Circumplex mood model is a dimensional framework used in psychology and affective computing to describe and mathematically measure human mood
. Rather than attempting to classify emotions into rigid, discrete categories—such as Ekman's basic emotions of happiness, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, and surprise—the Circumplex model maps emotional states along continuous axes
.
Rooted in the foundational dimensional emotion theories of Wilhelm Wundt, the standard Circumplex model relies on two primary dimensions to map a user's affect:
The Pleasure Dimension (Valence): This axis measures how positive or negative a person feels
.
The Activeness Dimension (Arousal): This axis measures whether a person is likely to take action under their current mood state, ranging from highly active to completely passive
.
By intersecting these two dimensions, the model creates a two-dimensional spatial graph. Any specific, complex emotional state can be plotted as a coordinate within this space
. For example, a state of "excitement" would be mapped as high-pleasure and high-activeness, whereas "contentment" would map as high-pleasure but low-activeness. Research demonstrates that individuals are able to accurately and consistently place their discrete feelings into this two-dimensional space
.
Advantages for Recommendation Systems Using the Circumplex model provides several major advantages over traditional categorical emotion models when building tracking and recommendation systems:
Mathematical Quantifiability: It is notoriously difficult to quantify discrete categorical emotions for computational algorithms
. Because the Circumplex model outputs direct, continuous numerical values along its two axes, it allows systems to easily translate mood into mathematical feature vectors
.
Comprehensive Coverage: Basic discrete categories fail to cover the full, nuanced range of human affect and make it hard to decide on a universal set of independent moods
. The Circumplex space fluidly accounts for a vast spectrum of transitional and blended mood states
.
Low User Friction: The model is highly valued because it is simple and quick to administer
. Since users only need to evaluate their current state across two highly intuitive metrics (how good they feel and how energetic they feel), it minimizes user fatigue. This makes it particularly effective for applications that require users to log their mood multiple times a day over long periods
.

what is LIWC and how does it work

Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC) is a highly popular linguistic resource and dictionary-based tool originally developed for psychological analysis
. While many text analysis tools focus purely on syntax or basic vocabulary, LIWC is uniquely designed to extract and quantify psychological, grammatical, and content-based word categories from a given text
.
How LIWC is Structured The core of the LIWC system (specifically the widely used LIWC 2015 version) is a massive dictionary containing over 6,400 terms, which includes full words, word stems, and even emoticons
. These terms are organized into roughly 92 to 94 distinct categories or sub-dictionaries
.
These categories range from simple grammatical classifications to deep psychological states:
Grammatical Categories: Some LIWC classes simply reflect basic parts of speech and are structurally straightforward. For instance, the "articles" dictionary only contains three words: "a", "an", and "the"
.
Psychological and Thematic Categories: Other classes are highly nuanced and are constructed by human experts to capture specific affective or cognitive states, such as "emotions", "social words", "perceptual processes" (words related to seeing, hearing, or feeling), and "biological processes"
.
Overlapping Definitions: The tool is designed to understand that words have layered meanings. A single word can belong to multiple dictionaries simultaneously; for example, a specific term could trigger a hit in both the "sadness" dictionary and the broader "emotions" dictionary
.
To ensure the psychological validity of these complex categories, LIWC relies on strict human curation. For a word to be added to or removed from an expert-constructed dictionary, an agreement of two out of three human judges is required
.
How LIWC Processes Text When you feed a text—such as the full epub of an audiobook—into LIWC, it works by systematically counting the occurrences of its dictionary terms within the target text.
Rather than just providing raw word counts, the final value LIWC outputs for any given category is the exact percentage of the text's total words that fall into that specific dictionary
. For example, if a book has a high reliance on passive language and helping verbs, LIWC will compute a precise metric, such as stating that exactly 4.54% of the words in the book are "auxiliary verbs"
. Similarly, it can output the precise percentage of words in the book dedicated to "affect words" or "latinate words"
.
Applications in Literature and Recommendation Systems Because LIWC translates abstract psychological themes and writing styles into concrete numerical percentages, it is highly effective for the types of non-story (stylometric) tracking you are building for your recommendation engine.
In computational literary analysis, LIWC has been successfully used to:
Profile Genres: Quantitative analysis using LIWC reveals distinct stylistic fingerprints across different genres. For instance, studies have shown that compared to mystery novels, science fiction books contain significantly more cognition and religious terms, but fewer social words, pronouns, and auxiliary verbs
. Conversely, fantasy literature contains significantly more biological and religious terms than mystery books
.
Profile Authors and Characters: LIWC features are frequently used to analyze the specific profiling of authors and to study the linguistic expression of fictional characters' genders
.
Drive Recommendation Algorithms: By extracting these deep semantic features (such as the percentage of words related to human "core drives and needs" or "perceptual processes"), you can build an exact 94-dimension numerical vector for any book
. As established in your system's design, this dense vector can then be compared against a user's personal preference profile to mathematically predict how much they will enjoy the stylistic and psychological makeup of the book.

What are the six core emotional arcs and their coefficients?

The emotional trajectories of stories can be mathematically categorized into six core emotional arcs, which form the fundamental building blocks of complex narrative trajectories
. These arcs are identified by running a sliding-window sentiment analysis over a text to create a raw emotional time-series, which is then decomposed using a mathematical technique called Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
.
When SVD is applied to a large corpus of books, it discovers the primary, independent emotional shapes (called "modes") that underline all narratives
. SVD inherently produces a mathematical matrix of mode coefficients, which are continuous numerical values that determine how strongly a single book aligns with each of the core emotional modes
.
Because SVD math produces both positive and negative coefficients, each of the three foundational SVD modes actually accounts for two opposite emotional arcs depending on the polarity of the coefficient
. To make these coefficients usable and account for the fact that different books possess varying amounts of total emotional "energy", the coefficients for each book are typically normalized so that they sum to 1
.
Here is the detailed breakdown of the six core emotional arcs and how their coefficients function:
1. The "Rags to Riches" Arc (A Steady Rise)
This arc is defined by a continuous, steady upward trajectory in fortune and positive sentiment
.
The Coefficient: This arc is represented by a positive coefficient for Mode 1
. Mode 1 is the most dominant emotional shape in literature, capturing roughly 30% of the entire variance across the corpus
.
Examples: Books with the highest positive coefficients for this mode include Shakespeare's The Winter's Tale and Oscar Wilde's Art and Morality: A Defence of ‘The Picture of Dorian Gray’
.
2. The "Tragedy" or "Riches to Rags" Arc (A Steady Fall)
This arc is the exact inverse of "Rags to riches," characterized by a continuous, steady downward trajectory into negative sentiment and despair
.
The Coefficient: This arc is represented by a negative coefficient for Mode 1
.
Examples: Books with strongly negative coefficients for this mode include Jane Austen's Lady Susan and, famously, Shakespeare's Romeo and Juliet
.
3. The "Man in a Hole" Arc (Fall then Rise)
Coined by Kurt Vonnegut, this highly popular narrative shape features a protagonist who falls into a difficult situation (a drop in sentiment) and then successfully climbs out of it (a rise in sentiment)
.
The Coefficient: This arc is represented by a positive coefficient for Mode 2
.
Examples: Stories that mathematically align most closely with this positive coefficient include The Magic of Oz and Children of the Frost
. Research indicates that variations of the "Man in a hole" arc are among the most commercially successful story shapes
.
4. The "Icarus" Arc (Rise then Fall)
This arc represents a character who experiences an initial rise in fortune or happiness, only to suffer a tragic downfall
.
The Coefficient: This arc is represented by a negative coefficient for Mode 2
.
Examples: Books with a strong negative Mode 2 coefficient include Shadowings and Battle-Pieces and Aspects of the War
. Alongside the "Man in a hole", the "Icarus" arc is also correlated with highly successful, frequently downloaded books
.
5. The "Cinderella" Arc (Rise, Fall, Rise)
This complex arc features a protagonist who experiences an initial rise in fortune, suffers a significant setback or crisis, and ultimately achieves a final, triumphant rise
.
The Coefficient: This arc is represented by a positive coefficient for Mode 3
.
Examples: Books exhibiting the highest positive coefficients for this shape include Mystery of the Hasty Arrow and Through the Magic Dorr
.
6. The "Oedipus" Arc (Fall, Rise, Fall)
This is the inverse of the Cinderella story, featuring a protagonist who experiences an initial fall, recovers to a high point, but ultimately suffers a final, devastating drop in fortune
.
The Coefficient: This arc is represented by a negative coefficient for Mode 3
.
Examples: Stories characterized by this negative coefficient include This World is Taboo, Old Indian Days, and The Evil Guest
.
How the Coefficients are Used in Practice In a computational recommendation system (such as your Windlass architecture), these normalized coefficients provide a massive advantage. Rather than relying on simple metadata tags, the system can reduce the entire emotional plot of a 100,000-word novel into a tiny, dense vector of just a few numbers ranging from -1 to 1
. By comparing a book's mode coefficients to a user's preference profile, the system can mathematically predict if a user is currently in the mood for the redemptive rise of a "Man in a hole" story or the catastrophic drop of a "Tragedy."
